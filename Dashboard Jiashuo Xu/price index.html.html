§ 01 — Policy Background

How Did We Get Here?

New York City's Central Business District Tolling Program (CBDTP) — popularly known as congestion pricing — is the first cordon-based urban road toll in the United States. Charging most vehicles that enter Manhattan south of 60th Street, it was designed simultaneously to reduce gridlock and generate dedicated funding for mass transit infrastructure.

The idea dates to 2007, when Mayor Michael Bloomberg proposed a similar scheme, but it failed to advance legislatively. A decade later, a transit crisis reignited the debate: the summer of 2017 saw a Q-train derailment, A-line track fires, and packed F-trains stranded in tunnels — leading Governor Cuomo to declare the system in a "state of emergency." The crisis made clear that the MTA's budget, untouched for nearly three decades, needed structural reform.

In 2019, the New York State Legislature included congestion pricing in the state budget under Article 44-C, the Traffic Mobility Act. Years of environmental review, legal battles, federal approvals, and political negotiations followed — including a 2024 pause ordered by Gov. Hochul — before the program finally activated at midnight on January 5, 2025.

"Early results are exactly consistent with what the congestion pricing models predicted."

— Charles Komanoff, Eno Center for Transportation Panel, April 2025

§ 02 — Traffic & Mobility Outcomes

Year One: Traffic Down Across the Region

By virtually every traffic metric, congestion pricing has delivered on its primary mandate. In Year 1, more than 27 million fewer vehicles entered Manhattan's Congestion Relief Zone (CRZ), representing an average daily reduction of 73,000 vehicles — an 11% drop compared to 2024 baseline levels.

Speed improvements were measurable across all road types. According to NBER Working Paper 33584, average speeds inside the CBD rose from 8.2 to 9.7 mph — a 15% improvement. During the most congested periods (weekday afternoons, 1–7 PM), speed gains exceeded 20%; on weekend evenings they exceeded 25%.

Perhaps more striking: traffic volumes fell even as Manhattan office occupancy rose. The Partnership for New York City reported that Manhattan office attendance reached 76% of pre-pandemic levels in March 2025, up from 72% in 2024 — suggesting drivers are genuinely switching to transit rather than simply staying home.

Safety Improvements

Traffic safety improvements have accompanied the speed and volume gains. Crashes within the CRZ declined by 7%, and traffic injuries fell 8% year-over-year. New York City as a whole recorded its fewest traffic deaths in history in 2025, down 19% from 2024 — the lowest since reliable records began.

Noise pollution also fell sharply. Vehicle noise complaints to NYC's 311 system declined 23% over the year, and honking complaints specifically dropped 45% in 2025 compared to 2024.

§ 03 — Environmental Impact

Air Quality, Emissions & Sustainability

PM2.5 Reduction: NYC vs. Global Peers
Sources: Cornell npj Clean Air (2025); Stockholm (2006–2010); London (2019–2022)

A landmark study published in npj Clean Air (December 2025) by Cornell University researchers analyzed 17,758 observations from 42 air quality monitors across 518 days. In the first six months of the program, average daily maximum PM2.5 concentrations in the CRZ declined by 3.05 micrograms per cubic meter — a reduction of 22% compared to the control period.

This surpasses results seen in other congestion pricing implementations globally. Stockholm's program reduced PM2.5 by 5–15% between 2006 and 2010; London saw a 7% drop between 2019 and 2022. Cornell's lead researcher attributed NYC's larger impact to the sheer density and scale of the city's population and traffic.

Crucially, the study found that pollution did not simply shift to surrounding areas — declines were observed across all five boroughs and into surrounding suburbs, suggesting commuters are genuinely choosing cleaner transportation modes rather than rerouting vehicles.

"This tells us congestion pricing didn't simply relocate air pollution to the suburbs. Instead, folks are choosing cleaner transportation options altogether."

— Prof. Fei Gao, Director, Cornell Center for Transportation, Environment and Community Health

§ 05 — Transit Investment

Funding New York's Transit Future

The second pillar of congestion pricing is transit funding. By bonding against a steady revenue stream, the MTA can access $15 billion in capital for its 2020–2024 capital plan — the largest transit capital program in New York State history.

Net toll revenue in 2025 reached an estimated $550 million — approximately $50 million above the original $500 million projection. Revenue breakdown in the early months showed 68% from passenger vehicles, 22% from taxis and for-hire vehicles, 9% from trucks, and 1% from buses and motorcycles.

As of January 2026, more than $6 billion in projects unlocked by congestion revenue are already in construction, with more to be awarded throughout 2026.

§ 06 — Ongoing Controversy & Debate

Political Landscape & Remaining Tensions

Despite broadly positive first-year data, congestion pricing remains politically contested. Federal opposition, ongoing litigation, and community-level equity concerns continue to shape the program's future.

Federal Opposition

President Trump declared "CONGESTION PRICING IS DEAD" in February 2025, with the USDOT revoking federal approval the same day. Gov. Hochul and the MTA filed suit immediately. A federal judge blocked federal retaliatory funding cuts in May 2025, and as of early 2026, tolling cameras remain operational while litigation continues.

Legal Landscape

The MTA has fought off at least nine lawsuits — from the Town of Hempstead, New Jersey, federal agencies, and various municipalities. Two cases remained pending as of January 2026. Courts have consistently allowed the program to continue operating.

Community & Equity Concerns

Some local officials, particularly from outer boroughs, continue to raise equity concerns. State Sen. Jessica Scarcella-Spanton (Staten Island/Brooklyn) cited disproportionate respiratory illness rates on Staten Island's North Shore as evidence of environmental injustice from traffic diversion.

The program includes mitigation measures: $20M for an asthma center in the Bronx, $15M to replace diesel refrigeration units at Hunts Point Market, $20M for electric truck charging, and $10M for air filtration in highway-adjacent schools.

Academic Consensus

Transportation researchers broadly view the program as a success by its stated metrics, while noting the $9 toll represents a political compromise below what economists would consider the optimal level to capture all external costs of driving.
